左边是圣三位一体：圣父在上方托举十字架上的基督，圣灵通常以鸽子象征，形成一条非常强的垂直轴线。下方跪着两位圣人：圣塞巴斯蒂安和圣洛克。这两位在传统中都和抵御瘟疫、疾病、城市灾厄有关；大都会的音频资料也提到，这件游行旗可能与 1497–1499 年间奇塔迪卡斯泰洛及翁布里亚地区的瘟疫背景有关，可被理解为一种还愿或祈祷性质的图像。

右边是《夏娃的创造》：亚当斜卧在地，天父俯身靠近他的身体，上方两个天使在空中飞翔。这个场景来自《创世记》：夏娃由亚当的肋骨被创造出来。它和左边的“十字架”放在一起时，形成了一个很典型的基督教神学对照：从创世到救赎，从亚当到基督，从人类的起源到人类的得救。

构图上，左幅非常稳定、庄严，几乎是“正面圣像式”的。十字架、圣父、基督身体构成中心轴，两位圣人像见证者一样跪在地面，天空、山石、树木都被安排得很安静。这种宁静、清澈、对称的气质，很接近佩鲁吉诺的翁布里亚风格：人物表情温柔，风景开阔，色彩柔和，宗教情绪不是激烈的痛苦，而是一种安静的祈祷。

Raphael’s works for private devotion reveal the beginnings of his slow transition away from Perugino’s models and toward his own distinctive vocabulary. Although the facial features and hand gesture of this Saint Sebastian recall the elder artist’s style, the figure set against the detailed landscape is more sculptural and the treatment of color and light dynamic and complex. Raphael may have been drawn to Florence after hearing fellow painters rave about rival full-scale designs (cartoons) for spectacular murals there by Leonardo and Michelangelo.

Related works in this gallery convey the electrifying novelty and arresting sculptural power of those unrealized projects.

While in Florence between 1504 and 1508, Raphael also studied Michelangelo’s sculptures and Leonardo’s paintings and drawings, promptly and seamlessly adapting aspects of their styles and techniques in his work.

Raphael also learned from his peers’ revolutionary approaches to inventing compositions. Leonardo’s theories about “sketching quickly,” in the fervor of artistic inspiration, transformed Raphael’s methods. Asserting that the effort to finish drawings and puzzle out details could dry up fresh ideas, Leonardo advised working out concepts in “rough sketches,” like a poet scrawling the first draft of a verse in messy handwriting with crossed-out words. Before Florence, Raphael had tended to develop compositions in a more artisanal way, figure by detailed figure, as he had learned from Perugino. Now, spontaneous sketching led him to more organic compositions pulsating with movement and vivid expression.

Leonardo may have invited Raphael to study his drawings, which would have shown him examples of a diversity of techniques and media. In this expressive sheet, which he started around 1490 and returned to more than a decade later, a frenzy of red-chalk sketches of galloping horsemen for Leonardo’s Battle of Anghiari mural lie over a carefully constructed study of male proportions, with notes on measurements that he also revised on the paper. The impressive sheet, driven by an almost feral energy, instantly communicates the freedom in Leonardo’s inspiration.

This sheet by Leonardo exemplifies the radically different approach to drawing that would prove so influential on Raphael. The artist packed into one small space a dazzling sequence of quick sketches outlining four ideas for altarpieces of the Virgin adoring the Christ Child, including, at center, the composition that would become the Virgin of the Rocks (which exists in two painted versions). It is an early and masterful example of Leonardo’s theory of “quick sketching” (bozzare pronto), the notion that figures could be sketched “very roughly” in the process of spontaneous drawing, letting multiple ideas pour onto the paper with freshness.

Raphael’s dazzling preliminary sketches for Virgin and Child paintings produced in Florence are especially suggestive of Leonardo’s method. Here, the ideas for the figures emerge without definitive outlines, conjuring grace and an electrifying energy of movement. Although the final painting that relates to the design at lower center is quite static, Raphael’s arresting early sketches capture the inchoate forms of the figures and the hints of their motion, as Leonardo would have desired.

Many of Leonardo’s theoretical exercises might have seemed excessive to the practically minded Raphael, whose training in Perugino’s workshop had instilled in him methodical design practices.

Yet it is clear from the younger artist’s drawings that he absorbed Leonardo’s teachings about the value of quick sketches and spontaneity. On this large sheet, Raphael vigorously sketched putti in various states of action and repose around a large study for the raised head of Saint Catherine. She looks upward in spiritual rapture, as in the full-scale drawing (cartoon) hanging nearby and the related painting (National Gallery, London).

This impressive portrayal of Saint Catherine of Alexandria is among the earliest surviving large cartoons that accord with Raphael’s new Florentine practices. The artist created the drawing with great freshness in black chalk (rather than pen and brown ink) in preparation for his related painting, illustrated below. Most cartoons were destroyed during the working process, which involved pricking holes in the paper, then rubbing it with pouncing dust to transfer the design to the painting surface; in order to spare the drawing displayed here, the design was instead pricked onto a “substitute cartoon.”

Although Leonardo’s original Leda and the Swan painting is lost, the composition had a profound effect on the artists in his circle, and on the young Raphael. This sixteenth-century copy was likely painted by two artists, one who executed the figures and another the dramatic Alpine landscape. The elegant, twisting pose of the central figure is one that Raphael drew upon several times, adapting it for his Saint Catherine (at left. Raphael’s drawings after this painting and Michelangelo’s sculptures demonstrate that he not only absorbed the influences of his forebears but also reeducated himself with tenacity in the grand new manner pioneered by the great artists of the moment.

This rare unfinished painting, produced around the time he left Florence for Rome, permits a glimpse into Raphael’s creative process. Portrait

1503

As in Leonardo’s Mona Lisa, the face and crossed hands in this portrait provide two strong focal points, both in a compositional sense and as gestures that manifest the “motions of the mind.” The woman may be Giovanna “Feltria” della Rovere (1463-1513), a patron of the arts whose husband had recently died. Her dark blue-gray eyes gaze in the viewer’s direction but do not fully connect, and the funereal background amplifies her aura of contemplation. The gesture of her left hand communicates a psychological intensity: she holds what may be a handkerchief or glove, and her tensed index finger presses hard against the bottom border of the composition, as if the edge of the painting were a physical ledge.

Among Raphael’s portraits, this luminous painting comes closest to espousing the influential ideals of beauty articulated in the poetry of Petrarch (1304-1374), who exalted women’s blond hair and pale skin. The small unicorn on her lap—a symbol of love and chastity-and her elegant costume and jewelry indicate that this was a betrothal or marriage portrait. Sumptuous materials advertised the wealth, status, and “value” of a bride, as a matter of honor for her family. The painting may represent Laura Orsini della Rovere (1492-1530/31), whose family emblem was the unicorn and whose mother was Giulia Farnese, a mistress of Pope Alexander VI admired for her beauty.

This painting presents a novel, naturalistic female type—a sitter in the guise of the goddess Venus.

It is also clearly a portrait of an individual. She looks directly at an observer and exhibits strong, distinctive features. The woman’s hands gesture toward her exposed breast and to her clothed pubic area, a quotation of an ancient Roman sculpture type called the Venus Pudica, or “modest Venus.” High on her left arm is a blue band with elaborate gold borders and the inscription “RAPHAEL VRBINAS,” a visual manifestation of the poetic bondage of love. The portrait is known as La Fornarina (the female baker) because it may represent Margherita Luti, Raphael’s mistress and the daughter of a baker.

Raphael’s disciplined approach to the design process emerges with clarity in his drawings connected with Marcantonio Raimondi’s engraving of the Massacre of the Innocents. The subjeet, from the Gospel of Matthew, is King Herod’s order that his soldiers slaughter all the male infants in Bethlehem. In Raphael’s hands, the story became an exercise in the rhetoric of cruelty and pathos full of violent action in the unfolding. The sheet of studies in red chalk, partly drawn from live models, portrays the nude soldier who anchors the design and three iterations of the terrified mother next to him. The two composition studies are in the same scale as the print. In the earlier one, Raphael sketched the figures quickly in pen and ink, in keeping with his practice of first visualizing a composition with all the figures rendered in the nude. The outlines are finely pricked to enable transfer of the design, which he continued to revise. His carefully elaborated final composition drawing was the last step before he or an assistant made a tracing on translucent paper, which they used to transfer the design to a copper plate by pricking the outlines with a needle or incising them onto the metal surface. The engraver would then take over, masterfully incising contours and carefully ordered strokes of hatching onto the copper plate used to produce the print.

As in other large-scale projects of his later years, Raphael produced this painting with the considerable help of his workshop, including his trusted assistant Giulio Romano. Scientific investigation and restoration have revealed fascinating details about this altarpiece, including the presence of a complex perspective grid under the paint layers and the way that Raphael slightly rotated the entire composition to move away from the symmetrical classicism of the first idea toward a more expressive and dramatic vision. This analysis has also clarified that the contribution of each workshop artist was not subdivided by areas or individual figures, but often followed one another, layer upon layer, with Raphael typically executing the final paint layers in order to harmonize the whole.

The prestige of Raphael’s Acts of the Apostles tapestries, commissioned by Pope Leo X and woven for the Sistine Chapel, was such that royal patrons ordered their own editions.

Part of a set owned by King Philip II of Spain (1527-1598), this one displays a composition that establishes the authority of the first pope. Christ says, “I will give you the keys of the kingdom of heaven” and points at both Peter and the grazing sheep, a reference to Peter’s role as shepherd of the faithful.

In this unusual and influential composition, the apostle Paul performs the miracle of converting his Roman jailers through an earthquake that causes the prison doors to open.

Raphael’s muscular personification of the earthquake not only disrupts the scene above but also breaks the tapestry border on the right side as he lunges toward the viewer. Part of the original set of tapestries commissioned by Pope Leo X and woven in Brussels for the Sistine Chapel, its unusual narrow shape was dictated by the layout of the screen and choir in the chapel, and it was produced only once.
