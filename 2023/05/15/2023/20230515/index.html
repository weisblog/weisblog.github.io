先给概括介绍他们的地理时代分布吧：

介绍下较不熟悉的TEOTIHUACAN：

Teotihuacan controlled the central highlands of Mexico from around AD 100 to 650, its influence extending south to Costa Rica and northwards as far as New Mexico in the United States. Located northeast of Mexico City, Teotihuacan was one of the world’s biggest cities in the 5th century AD, with as many as 120,000 people. The Pyramid of the Sun, which sits along a great ceremonial axis running through the center of Teotihuacan, is one of the largest structures of the ancient world, rivaling even the pyramids at Giza in Egypt.

Replicating the mythical Mountain of Creation, the pyramid was the city’s center of religious and political activities.

The metropolis was burned and abandoned in the 7th century, but its memory survived. Eight centuries later, the Aztecs believed the gods gathered there to create the universe because the ruined city was so grand. The Aztecs named the city Teotihuacan, meaning Place of the Gods. We do not know the city’s ancient name or that of its people.


再说说他们（Maya）的宗教系统，文字有点多，简单标出重点。

The gods created the tri-level Maya universe -heavens, earth, and under-world-when they erected the world-tree in the center of Three-Stone-Place, the cosmic hearth. The world-tree (which is based on the ceiba tree, found throughout Central America), also served as a supernatural road connecting these realms.

The tree pierces Flower Mountain, at the center of the disk-like earth. The mountain contains the cave of origin, from which maize and the four ancestral couples first emerged. The cave is also the entrance to the underworld, a watery realm inhabited by bizarre deities and malevolent spirits. The human soul passed through this perilous place on its journey to resurrection, when the soul became a divine ancestor.

下面是几幅作品：

Earthenware with post-fire paint

The Maize god, who sits atop this vase’s lid, symbolizes life after death, as the appearance of maize in the fields each spring was the Maya metaphor for the resurrection of the human soul. The living made offerings to the dead through the lid’s opening, which also served as a conduit for communication with the gods and deified ancestors.

Large burial urns like these are unique to the K’iché Maya of Guatemala’s southern high-lands, and nearly all date to the two centuries between AD 650 and 850. These urns held the remains of important people and were buried in pyramids or placed in sacred caves, where descendants made offerings and sought advice from their revered ancestors.

Guatemala (Motul de San José are), AD 750-800

This is one of the supreme examples of Maya vase painting. Look for the subtle color washes on the figures’ bodies, a watercolor-like effect that is extremely difficult to achieve with slip paint. The vase’s scene relates to the core Mesoamerican principle of sacrifice and rebirth to renew the world’s fertility. The artist coflated two seminal myths- the sacrifice of the Baby Jaguar deity and the birth/resurrection of the Maize god-to express this universal cycle. The supine jaguar deity’s white umbilical cord unifies the wrap-around scene.

BLOOD SACRIFICE AND THE COSMIC BALANCE

Maintaining the balance of the universe was a key principle of Maya religious and political beliefs. This was achieved by blood sacrifice. The gods had sacrificed their own blood to infuse the cosmos with life; they, in turn, needed similar nourishment from humans. In response, the gods provided life-giving rain to sustain people and crops, and ensured the natural cycle of the earth.

Guatemala (Motul de San José), AD 735-768

Amassing tribute and celebrating alliances with foreign lords was among a ruler’s most important political duties. Ritual performances, with religious overtones, publically proclaimed these relations and their sacred undertones. Here the ruler Yajawte’ K’inich performs a personal blood sacrifice on November 20, 743 and dances with allies from Hix Witz and Tayasal, two dynamic polities of the time. The performers wear costumes portraying supernatural beings while the ruler seemingly rides a jaguar, its rear legs arching over his head. The jaguar’s front legs mimic those of Yajawte’ K’inich, suggesting a merging of man and spirit entity during the rite. （与巫师有关）

THE HACHA

The function of the misnamed hacha (“axe” in Spanish) remains a mystery, although ballplayer images on stone monuments depict them as equipment worn by players. However, the heavy stone versions may have been trophies or other ritual objects, often found in burials and ritual caches.

Seated man, Late Formative to Early Classic periods, 300 BC-AD 200

Cradling a game ball, this player wears a wide hip wrap, and a vertical ring elongates his nose. The torn earlobes suggest sacrificial blood offerings made by cutting the earlobe, a common practice 1,300 years later among the Aztecs and witnessed by the Spanish.

唯一一件Aztec的展品，风格可爱很多

Mask, Mexico, Mixtec or Aztec (Mexica), AD 1400-1521

Masks imparted identity to bundled funerary remains and to living performers who took the guise of other beings, both human and divine. This rare wooden mask was originally covered with an intricate mosaic of shell and colored stones including turquoise, imported from northern Mexico or New Mexico. Its artistry suggests a Mixtec origin (Oaxaca or Puebla) whereas its style is Aztec

FEMALE FIGURES (Lagunillas style)

The Lagunillas style, formerly called “Chinesco” (Spanish for”Chinese-like”) because of its Asiatic facial traits, features introspective portrayals of young women. Their standard pose -with bare breasts, exposed genitalia, and hands on the abdomen -focus attention on women’s procreative powers and responsibility as bearers of children. All are seated on the ground in contact with the earth, recalling archetypal Mesoamerican beliefs concerning the earth as primordial mother and child-birthing in close proximity to mother earth. The figures’ nudity and youthfulness suggest their formal presentation as young adults ready to assume their maternal role.

Teotihuacan Articulated human effigy AD 350-550

Teotihuacan artists produced figurines in myriad forms and sizes. Larger, hollow sculptures with moveable limbs like this one have been found in burials and ceremonial caches. They are typically female or non-sexed, and likely were originally dressed in clothing signifying their identity and meaning, both of which remain unknown today.

Drinking vase for kakaw (chocolate), Guatemala, AD 650-800

Ten men drink an alcoholic brew and smoke potent tobacco as part of a ritual intoxication event. The drink is contained in jars marked with the hieroglyph chih, “pulque” (fermented agave sap). The text around the vase’s rim, however, states that it was the kakaw (choco-late) drinking vessel of a Maya noble. The shorter texts identify the participants and include the glyph “drunken one” for the inebriated man supported by two attendants and the one seated in front of the large jar with agave leaves on its rim.

最后一件算是最为精细的了：

Crocodile effigy incense burner
Costa Rica or Nicaragua (Greater Nicoya region), AD 400-1350

The form of this incense burner mimics the geography of Central America. The crocodile’s jagged back simulates the continent’s mountainous spine punctuated by smoking volcanoes. Smoldering coals and incense were placed in the burner’s base; aromatic smoke exiting through the crocodile’s body and mouth would imitate a volcanic eruption.
